Project Description

The web-map is developed within the PhD research project “Climate change resilient electrification of island communities in Southeast Asia”. The project aims to integrate climate change risk considerations into off-grid energy system planning. This is a first approach to facilitate the usage and application of gathered data for resilient energy system planning. The web-map provides an overview of climate change risks occurring on Southeast Asian islands and gives recommendations how to mitigate these risks by suggesting adaptation measures for system components. It also includes estimations of cost structures to implement these measures and demand increases based on climate change effects. The results (adjusted investment cost per system component and increased demand) may serve as input parameters to common energy system planning tools. More information

2.2. Data Processing

The underlying open-access climate change data was analyzed using open-source tools (QGIS and R).

Temperature

Temperature development is assessed based on the difference in current (1960 – 1990) and future (2040 – 2060) temperature data. The unit is given in degree Celsius.

Flood and Drought

To analyze flood and drought risk, the Hydrological Drought Index (HDI) as developed by the Satellite Hydrology Project (SATH) was calculated based on current and future temperature and precipitation data as well as extraterrestrial radiation and soil water balance data. The index has no unit and an advanced scale is applied:

HDI Classification
<= 0.5 high drought risk
< 0.5 - 0.75 moderate drought risk
< 0.7 - 1 slight drought risk
<1 - 1.25 slight flood risk
< 1.25 - 1.5 moderate flood risk
< 1.5 slight flood risk
Sea-Level Rise

Land loss is calculated as the percentage of land under water based on the CoastalDEM elevation model and sea-level rise projection of 0.26 m.

Cyclone

Cyclone risk is given in number of cyclones likely to occur within the next 50 years.

Risk Cluster

Clustering is a method of analyzing large datasets by dividing them into various clusters, thus helping to recognize patterns in the dataset. The cluster analysis conducted in the previous research project acted as an aid to understand how climate risks were spatially distributed and if risk patterns could be recognized, to easily find out how big the influence of the climate risks for the islands were, or whether clusters were defined by an interaction of varying climate risks. Therefore, the clusters were not necessarily geographically connected, even though they had similar characteristics.

The islands grouped in the first cluster were located at the West and East edge of SEA mainly in the Philippines. These islands border directly the Indian and Pacific Ocean. Secondly, they are located predominantly outside 5° tropic corridor and were strongly affected by tropical cyclones. The tropical cyclones cause winter monsoon, with maximal precipitation between in winter.

The islands in the second cluster were mainly located in the inner tropics and south of it. Only few islands were located as north as in the Bay of Bengal. The average island size was smaller than in the other two clusters. The second cluster involved grouping predominantly small islands and few larger islands and were characterized by a low flood risk and low risk of tropical cyclones, yet the temperature increase was slightly higher than the first cluster.

High precipitation and high flood risk were dominant in the third cluster. A few islands were situated outside of influence of monsoon areas. Those islands were the largest in SEA, like Borneo, Sumatra, Java, and West Papua. These islands were topographically characterized by higher altitudes, mountains, and volcanos, resulting in higher precipitation than in surrounding areas. The big islands of the third cluster were mostly located in the inner tropics.

Expert Interview

The list of potential adaptation measures as well as increased demand to reflect impacts of climate change were obtained by conducting expert interviews. In total 22 interviews were held with people of different backgrounds: project developers, technology providers, academia, insurance companies, island communities, energy cooperatives and energy policy. The goal of the interviews was to get a broad picture of both the implications of climate energy systems and the potential for adaptation.

After deriving the data from the expert interviews, the data was evaluated, and a compilation was made comprising of advisable adaptation measures on system components tailored to a particular climate risk. The cost of the adaptation measure was also evaluated, and a percentage was determined. This percentage value refers to a percentage of the initial investment cost to implement the respective system components. The information being passed across is that it would take the initial investment cost for a particular system component plus the specified percentage (for a particular climate risk) of the initial investment cost to make the system component resilient.
